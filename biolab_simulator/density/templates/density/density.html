Density

About

Knowledge of the density of a fuel is an extremely important factor in determining whether there will be good operation of an engine. The power produced by the engine is directly proportional to the mass of fuel burned. As the injection pump feeds constant volumes of diesel oil to the engine, the density influences the injected mass which, in turn, defines the power produced and the specific volumetric consumption of diesel oil. So the density must be controlled in a range to avoid variations in power for the same volume injected into the chamber (FARAH, 2015).

According to ANP's (Brazilian National Agency of Petroleum) specifications, this density range at 20°C covers values ​​ from 850 to 900 kg/m³. As previously described, within the allowed range, a higher density reduces specific consumption and increases the power generated, however, increases CO, hydrocarbon and particulate emissions, which are harmful to the environment.

Overall, temperature, pressure and composition of biodiesel are the main factors that determine its density, so that the higher the temperature, or the lower the pressure, the higher the density, thus, there are in the literature correlations that determine the density from these parameters (CHHETRI and WATTS, 2012). Another type of model widely used for this purpose is based on Equations of State (EoS), such as the Equation of State of Translated Volume (VTPR EoS) (SCHMID and GMEHLING, 2010).

There are also models based exclusively on the molecular structure of the components of a liquid to predict its molar volume, which makes up the contribution methods of GCVOL groups, from which these models are used to determine the biodiesel density over a temperature range. The first method to adopt this strategy (ELBRO, FREDENSLUND and RASMUSSEN, 1991), showed great accuracy, later suffering some new modifications with updates of the parameter values ​​for the contributing groups (PRATAS, FREITAS, et al., 2011).

Finally, another model widely used to predict the biodiesel density takes into account the structure of the compound, but with a different approach than group contribution methods. The strategy consists in evaluating the chain length, which is, the number of carbons present in the compound and the number of unsaturations present in the molecule (LAPUERTA, RODRÍGUEZ-FERNÁNDEZ and ARMAS, 2010), thus being able to apply mixing rules in the case of biodiesel, since it is a mixture of several esters.
